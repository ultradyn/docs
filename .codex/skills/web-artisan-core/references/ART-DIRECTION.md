# Art direction

## The thesis

Write one sentence that connects subject, audience, and visual behavior:

> For **[audience]** doing **[job]**, the interface feels like **[specific material/place/instrument/editorial lineage]**, expressed through **[layout/type/color/motion choices]**, so **[desired effect]**.

“Modern, clean, intuitive” is not a thesis. It cannot reject a bad idea.

## Ground it in the subject

Mine the subject's own world:

- materials and tools;
- environments and rituals;
- diagrams, notation, labels, and artifacts;
- language and cadence;
- emotional stakes;
- historical or cultural references;
- what expert users notice that outsiders miss.

Use those facts to shape content hierarchy, type, iconography, color, motion, and interaction. Do not merely decorate with topical illustrations.

## References and anti-references

For each reference, extract properties rather than copying a whole style:

- hierarchy and focal sequence;
- grid, alignment, and density;
- type roles and contrast;
- palette structure and semantic color;
- edge geometry and surface treatment;
- image crop/material;
- motion cadence;
- interaction model;
- what makes it feel authored.

For each anti-reference, state the property to avoid and the positive replacement. Example:

- Avoid equal-weight rounded cards → use one continuous workspace with rules, alignment, and typographic grouping.
- Avoid generic neon gradient hero → use a subject-specific live instrument as the focal point.

Positive steering is primary. Guardrails exist only where a common failure would otherwise recur.

## Signature move

Choose exactly one memorable element that embodies the thesis:

- a live product demonstration;
- a distinctive editorial grid;
- a subject-specific interaction;
- a carefully staged transition;
- a remarkable data view;
- a material image treatment;
- a typographic composition;
- an unusual but useful navigation model.

The signature move must serve the page job. If removing it improves comprehension, it was decoration.

## Typography

Define roles, not merely font names:

- display/hero;
- body/reading;
- utility/navigation;
- data/code/monospace where needed.

Specify fallback behavior, optical sizes if supported, measure, line height, weight range, tracking, numeral style, and which role carries personality. One type family may cover several roles when that is a deliberate choice.

## Color

Define semantic tokens first: canvas, surfaces, text tiers, border, accent, success, warning, danger, focus, selection, code/data series. Then map theme-specific values. Use accent where it signals action, identity, state, or hierarchy—not because a section is empty.

## Geometry and material

Specify radius scale, border/rule use, elevation model, shadow softness, surface opacity, and where containers are actually needed. Use alignment, whitespace, type, and dividers before adding another card.

## Motion

Choose a motion thesis:

- spatial explanation;
- direct manipulation feedback;
- orchestration/reveal;
- ambient subject atmosphere;
- restrained utility only.

Prefer one orchestrated moment to generic animation on every section. Define duration bands, easing character, interruption, and reduced-motion behavior.

## Self-critique

Before coding, ask:

1. Could this exact direction fit five unrelated AI/SaaS products?
2. Did a named style substitute for reasoning?
3. Is the hierarchy structural or made from boxes?
4. Is the signature move specific and useful?
5. Are type, color, and motion expressing the same thesis?
6. Does the direction remain coherent in both light and dark themes?

Revise any answer that exposes a generic choice.
